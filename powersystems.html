Compare thermal, hydro, and nuclear power plants in terms of their advantages and limitations

‚ñº

Thermal Power Plants:

  • Advantages: Can be located anywhere (fuel transportable), quick start-up, high reliability
  • Limitations: High fuel cost, pollution (CO‚ÇÇ, SO‚Çì, NO‚Çì), lower efficiency (35-40%), requires cooling water
  • Capital Cost: Moderate

Hydroelectric Power Plants:

  • Advantages: Renewable, zero emissions, quick response to load changes, long lifespan, high efficiency (85-90%)
  • Limitations: Site-specific (requires suitable topography), high initial cost, environmental impact (flooding), seasonal variations
  • Capital Cost: High

Nuclear Power Plants:

  • Advantages: High energy density, low fuel cost per unit energy, zero greenhouse gases during operation
  • Limitations: Radioactive waste disposal, safety concerns, high capital cost, public opposition, limited fuel availability
  • Capital Cost: Very high

Economic Dispatch: Hydro plants typically have the lowest operating cost, followed by nuclear, then thermal plants.

What are the differences between short, medium, and long transmission lines?

‚ñº

Transmission lines are classified based on their length and the analysis method used:

Classification by Length:

Short Line: Length ‚â§ 80 km (50 miles)

Medium Line: 80 km < Length ‚â§ 240 km (150 miles)

Long Line: Length > 240 km

Short Transmission Lines:

  • Only series impedance (R + jX) is considered
  • Shunt capacitance is neglected
  • Voltage regulation is primarily affected by resistance and reactance
  • Used for distribution networks

Medium Transmission Lines:

  • Both series impedance and shunt capacitance are considered
  • Analyzed using nominal-œÄ or nominal-T equivalent circuits
  • Shunt capacitance is lumped at one or two points
  • Used for sub-transmission networks

Long Transmission Lines:

  • Parameters are distributed uniformly along the line
  • Analyzed using exact transmission line equations (hyperbolic functions)
  • Requires consideration of wave propagation effects
  • Used for bulk power transmission over long distances
  • Important phenomena: Ferranti effect, corona loss, surge impedance loading

Key Parameter: The classification depends on the electrical length (Œª/10) rather than just physical length.

Explain the importance of load curves and discuss different types of tariffs

‚ñº

Load Curves: Graphical representation of load variation versus time.

  • Daily Load Curve: Shows variation over 24 hours
  • Monthly Load Curve: Shows variation over a month
  • Yearly Load Curve: Shows seasonal variations

Important Parameters from Load Curves:

1. Maximum Demand (MD): Peak load during a period

2. Average Load = (Area under load curve) / Time period

3. Load Factor = (Average Load) / (Maximum Demand)

4. Diversity Factor = (Sum of individual max demands) / (System max demand)

5. Plant Capacity Factor = (Actual energy produced) / (Max possible energy)

Types of Tariffs:

1. Simple Rate (Flat Rate):

  • Fixed price per kWh consumed
  • Simple but doesn't encourage off-peak usage

2. Block Rate:

  • Different rates for different consumption blocks
  • Lower rates for higher consumption blocks (declining block rate)
  • Or higher rates for higher consumption (increasing block rate)

3. Two-Part Tariff:

Total Charges = Fixed Charge + Running Charge

Fixed Charge = a √ó kW of max demand

Running Charge = b √ó kWh consumed

4. Three-Part Tariff:

Total Charges = Fixed Charge + Semi-fixed Charge + Running Charge

Fixed Charge = Annual fixed cost

Semi-fixed = a √ó kW of max demand

Running Charge = b √ó kWh consumed

5. Time-of-Use (TOU) Tariff:

  • Different rates for peak, off-peak, and shoulder periods
  • Encourages load shifting to off-peak hours

What is the Ferranti effect in transmission lines and how can it be mitigated?

‚ñº

Ferranti Effect: A phenomenon in long transmission lines where the receiving-end voltage becomes higher than the sending-end voltage when the line is lightly loaded or open-circuited.

Cause: Charging current (Ic) flowing through the line capacitance

VR > VS when line is lightly loaded

Magnitude increases with line length and voltage level

Explanation:

  • In long lines, shunt capacitance is significant
  • When line is lightly loaded or no-load, current is mainly capacitive (charging current)
  • This capacitive current flows through series inductance, causing voltage rise
  • The effect is more pronounced in EHV (Extra High Voltage) lines

Problems Caused:

  • Overvoltage at receiving end can damage equipment
  • Insulation breakdown
  • Increased corona losses
  • Magnetic saturation in transformers
  • Reduced lifespan of equipment

Mitigation Methods:

  • Shunt Reactors: Connected at receiving end to absorb reactive power
  • Series Capacitors: Compensate line inductance
  • Static VAR Compensators (SVC): Fast reactive power control
  • Synchronous Condensers: Provide variable reactive power absorption
  • Load Switching: Connect suitable load to draw lagging current
  • Voltage Regulators: Tap-changing transformers
  • FACTS Devices: STATCOM, TCSC for dynamic compensation

Rule of Thumb: Ferranti effect becomes significant for lines longer than 240 km at 50 Hz or 200 km at 60 Hz.

Compare EHVAC and HVDC transmission systems

‚ñº

EHVAC (Extra High Voltage AC): AC transmission at voltages above 230 kV (typically 400 kV, 765 kV)

HVDC (High Voltage DC): DC transmission at high voltages (typically ¬±500 kV, ¬±800 kV)

Comparison:

1. Power Transfer Capacity:

HVDC: Higher for same conductor size (no skin effect, no charging current)

EHVAC: Limited by stability and reactive power requirements

2. Line Cost:

HVDC: Lower (fewer conductors, smaller towers)

EHVAC: Higher (more conductors, larger towers)

3. Terminal Cost:

HVDC: Very high (converters, filters, control systems)

EHVAC: Lower (transformers, circuit breakers)

4. Transmission Distance:

HVDC: Economical for > 500-800 km (break-even distance)

EHVAC: Economical for shorter distances

5. Power Losses:

  • HVDC: Lower line losses (no skin effect, no dielectric losses)
  • EHVAC: Higher losses (skin effect, corona, dielectric losses)

6. Interconnection:

  • HVDC: Can connect asynchronous AC systems, controls power flow
  • EHVAC: Requires synchronization, limited control over power flow

7. Fault Current:

  • HVDC: Lower fault current, easier to interrupt
  • EHVAC: Higher fault current, requires heavy circuit breakers

Applications:

  • HVDC: Long-distance bulk power transmission, submarine cables, asynchronous interconnections, renewable energy integration
  • EHVAC: Shorter distances, existing AC grid expansion, regional transmission

Modern Trend: Ultra HVDC (¬±800 kV, ¬±1100 kV) for mega power transmission projects.

What is the significance of per unit system in power system analysis?

Per Unit System: A method of expressing electrical quantities as fractions of a defined base unit, making calculations simpler in power systems.

Per Unit Value = (Actual Value) / (Base Value)

pu = V / Vbase for voltage

pu = I / Ibase for current

pu = S / Sbase for power

pu = Z / Zbase for impedance

  • Simplified Calculations: Eliminates ‚àö3 factors in three-phase systems
  • Easy Comparison: Values typically range between 0.1 and 10 pu

  • Voltage Level Independence: Same pu values on both sides of transformer
